The dominant abstraction for multi-agent AI — across orchestrators, supervisor patterns, agent graphs, message-passing protocols — is graph-shaped. Agents are nodes. Messages are edges. Coordination is traffic. The unit of observation is the message; the unit of intervention is the agent. This is a faithful discretization of hierarchical orchestration: there is in fact a planner, in fact an edge to each worker, in fact a turn-based protocol. It is the wrong discretization for everything else now being deployed under the same name.

What is actually being shipped is closer to a population of always-on processes sharing a common context, reading and writing into each other's working memory, with no clean turn boundary anywhere in the system. Query → response → silence is giving way to continuous information exchange. The interesting failure modes — echo, lock-in, collective drift, premature attractor closure, fragmentation into mutually unintelligible sub-populations — are not properties of any edge. They are properties of the medium the agents are coupled through. A graph + message-passing model renders them, at best, as statistical patterns reconstructed from output traces. Observability as forensics: examine the record after the fact.

I've spent the past year building memory infrastructure for AI agents — the [Recurse](https://recurse.cc) / [RAGE](/concepts/rage) project — and the move into multi-agent settings made this fault line hard to ignore. When the system you're instrumenting consists of agents that don't wait to be queried, that maintain ongoing state, that act on each other's activity as it happens rather than at well-defined turn boundaries, the trace-based instruments quietly stop reporting what is going on. The argument here is that the right model for the medium is a *continuous coupled dynamical system* — a substrate with its own physics, in which discrete-looking interactions are limit cases of an underlying field. This is not a metaphorical move. It changes what counts as an observable, what counts as a unit of analysis, and what counts as an intervention surface. It builds on a longer thread: [Divergence Engines](/articles/divergence-engines) on why similarity-optimized retrieval architectures collapse into sameness, and [How We May Think We Think](/articles/how-we-may-think-we-think) on cognition as recursive cycles between divergence and convergence. The substrate is the layer those arguments needed and didn't yet have.

## Frames as the recursive primitive

A complex adaptive system that nests at multiple scales — claim, session, territory, user, agent population, community — needs a single composable unit, or its dynamics have to be modeled separately at each level of nesting and substrate-wide properties become unobservable across scales. The frame is that unit. Not the everyday sense of "framing"; the technical sense Minsky gave the term. A typed semantic unit with named slots, defaults, attached procedures, and the ability to compose. A claim, a piece of evidence, a method, a problem, a position, a decision — whatever the content carries, plus its connective tissue. Fillmore's frame semantics is the linguistic complement: meaning lives not in isolated symbols but in the structured situations they invoke.

The load-bearing property is recursive composition. A paragraph is a frame. A document made of paragraphs is a frame. A session made of documents is a frame. A territory made of sessions is a frame. A user is a frame; an agent is a frame. The differences are scale, dynamism, and embodiment — not kind. Each is a configuration of accumulated abstractions of experience. A paragraph is a small, mostly static world model frozen in language. A report is a larger, aggregated one. An agent is a more active world model that updates as it interacts. A human is the most active and most embodied. Treating them as instances of one composition mechanism is what makes substrate-wide dynamics tractable; it is what makes the framing scale-invariant in the technical sense, rather than merely analogous across scales.

Two distinct claims sit under "scale-invariant" worth holding apart. *Compositional* uniformity — frame inside frame inside frame at every nesting level — is what the composition rule gives by construction. *Dynamic* scale-invariance — power-law activation cascades, 1/f spectra, the same exponents at every nesting depth — is an empirical claim about how activity propagates through the medium. The architectural commitment buys the *possibility* of fractal dynamics; the dynamics themselves have to be measured. The neuronal-avalanche literature is the closest precedent for nailing the same claim in cortex (Beggs & Plenz 2003; Shew & Plenz 2013), and it required careful statistics on avalanche-size distributions, branching ratios, and 1/f spectra to land. The substrate's vision is compatible with fractal dynamics in the technical sense; the distinction is worth naming because it tells you what the supporting measurements would look like.

This recursive uniformity is the precondition for fractal observability. A sub-agent of a sub-agent of a sub-agent that has quietly fallen into circular reasoning is structurally invisible to an orchestrator looking at top-level outputs; the orchestration boundary is opaque from above. The same pathology, in a substrate where every nesting level shares a primitive, is a closed circuit in the field — visible regardless of depth. The substrate runs one set of dynamics through one substance.

## Attention, action, and Hebbian coupling

Differential salience is a precondition for any complex adaptive system: some material has to be more present, more reachable, more influential than other material at any given moment, or there is nothing for the system to act on. Attention and action together generate that gradient. Attended material activates; acted-on material activates and reinforces; unattended material decays. Attention without action is observation; action without attention is reflex. The joint gradient is the field over which everything else plays out.

Structure on the substrate is *seeded by content and evolved by co-activation*, not imposed by schema. The seeding is geometric: each frame's initial position in phase space, its natural frequency, and its low-dimensional coordinates are derived deterministically from the embedding of its content. Two frames with semantically similar content land in similar regions of the field before any interaction has occurred. Co-activation does the rest: frames jointly attended to or acted on accumulate mutual influence; the strength grows with use and decays without it. Hebb's rule generalized to the medium — a positive-feedback dynamic on coupling, paired with the negative-feedback complement of decay. The pair is what keeps the substrate from runaway reinforcement on one side or signal evaporation on the other.

The semantic prior is what makes the substrate software-tractable in a way biological stigmergy isn't. An ant colony has to bootstrap shared structure from co-activation alone, which is why the cold-start regime is famously the hard part of the biological model. A substrate-mediated software system gets a head start: semantically related frames are already coordinated in phase space at t=0, and Hebbian dynamics evolve coupling on top of that initial geometry rather than constructing it from scratch. The architectural option that obtains here is neither schema nor pure emergence; it is content-derived initial geometry with interaction-driven evolution on top.

The coupling is not a static edge added to a graph. It is a continuous strength that ebbs and flows under use. Any graph one could draw at a given instant is a snapshot of an underlying continuous process — a freeze-frame of coupling strengths that are themselves moving. Decay constants in this concept are normalized to the rhythms of the agents and the territory rather than to wall clock time, because what counts as "stale" depends on the cadence of the work. The substrate's structural resistance to lock-in lives here: an attractor that no agent attends to anymore decays, regardless of how dominant it once was. The forgetting curve is baked into the geometry.

## Phase signatures as emergent identity

Each frame and each agent develops a high-dimensional, continuous, evolving signature with three contributors operating together. The first is semantic: initial phase is a deterministic projection of the frame's content embedding, which means semantically similar content lands in similar regions of phase space before any interaction has occurred. The second is explicit assignment: a new agent ingests its mission documents and working context as frames with their semantic-derived phase profile attached, so the agent already sits in a meaningful region of phase space before its first turn. The third is co-activation history: frequency clustering drifts the agent's effective phase over time as a function of what it has been jointly attended to. The shape is *seeded by semantic similarity and explicit assignment, then evolved by interaction*, not summoned out of activation history alone.

Once that distinction is clear, the rest of the picture lands. Frames with similar signatures sit close in the field. Agents with similar signatures coordinate through the substrate without explicit messaging. Agents with orthogonal signatures cannot easily exchange context, even when nominally in the same conversation. Identity is what the substrate carries and updates, not what the substrate requires up front and not what it manufactures from nothing — a continuous geometry seeded by content and shaped by use. (The agent-level signature is derived: an aggregate over the frames the agent has been recently active in. When the substrate "moves" an agent in phase space, what is moving in the underlying representation is the cluster of frames currently carrying that agent's attention.)

The empirical anchor in cortex is Atasoy and colleagues' connectome-harmonics framework: eigenmodes of the structural connectome's graph Laplacian as the natural basis for cortical activity, with low-frequency modes capturing global smooth patterns and high-frequency modes capturing local fine-grained ones. Substrate state, in the same posture, is field activity over a harmonic basis grounded in the substrate's coupling structure. The lineage holds with one clarification worth being precise about. Atasoy's decomposition earns its eigenmodes because the connectome is approximately stationary on the timescale of the activity it carries — fixed white-matter wiring, dynamics 4–6 orders of magnitude faster. The substrate's coupling, by design, is Hebbian-dynamic: frames co-activated this turn modify next-turn coupling. Computing meaningful eigenmodes of a moving Laplacian needs either a slow-windowed snapshot (couplings averaged over an N-turn window, N chosen for mode stability) or a different formalism altogether — the line of work on *adaptive networks of phase oscillators* (Aoki & Aoyagi 2009; Berner, Vock, Schöll, Yanchuk 2021) that handles co-evolving coupling and oscillator state explicitly. Connectome harmonics is the right anchor for the *spatial structure* of substrate activity. Adaptive networks of oscillators is the closer formal home for the *coupling-and-state-co-evolving* dynamics. The substrate's vision sits across both.

## Push, not pull

Most current "AI memory" architectures operate on a single rhythm: query, traverse, retrieve, respond. The agent stands outside a store and reaches in. Even sophisticated systems — graph retrieval, hierarchical chunking, hybrid sparse-dense indexes — share this gesture. Retrieval is the verb. The agent decides what to ask; the store delivers what was asked.

A substrate-mediated approach inverts the rhythm. Knowledge is not a store. It is a field of past activation, all of it alive at some level, with attention gradients propagating material toward where it is needed. Context arrives pre-assembled. Related material surfaces ahead of explicit request. Decay performs the function relevance ranking performs in pull-based systems — a property of the field, distributed across the agents acting through it, rather than a hidden function inside a service.

When N agents share a substrate, push-not-pull means they are not querying the same store independently — they are immersed in a shared cognitive field. Activation by one agent raises ambient salience for others. Decay for one is decay for the field. Coordination becomes implicit in the medium rather than something the agents perform via explicit message exchange.

Heylighen's framework distinguishes *quantitative* stigmergy — gradient-following on continuous markers, the ant pheromone case — from *qualitative* stigmergy, where agents act on typed traces interpreted in context, the termite-mound-stage case. The substrate is hybrid: phase signatures and decay are quantitative; typed frames with slots are qualitative. That hybrid is itself the architectural move worth naming. Biological stigmergy mostly lives at one pole or the other. The substrate's claim to novelty isn't "software stigmergy" — Dorigo's Ant Colony Optimization has thirty years of production deployment, and blackboard architectures (Hayes-Roth 1985) are arguably a third precedent. The genuinely new thing is *typed-semantic-frame stigmergy among LLM-class agents, with continuous phase coupling and Hebbian-evolving structure*. That is the regime current software didn't have a medium for.

Pull regimes synchronize on events. Push regimes synchronize on phase.

## Continuous coupled dynamical system

A substrate-mediated multi-agent system is closer in character to a coupled-oscillator network or a fluid than to a graph. Agents occupy positions in a phase space. Information has density gradients. Coordination is field interaction: phase coherence, flow patterns, attractor formation. The dynamics that matter — synchronization, lock-in, propagation cascades, drift, competitive dynamics among sub-populations — are dynamical phenomena, with natural descriptions in the language of fields rather than edges.

The toolkit follows. Kuramoto's coupled-oscillator framework gives the canonical formalism for collective state as a field property: an order parameter that captures the degree of phase coherence across a population, a coupling constant that controls the threshold for synchronization, a continuous account of how local interaction produces global phase locking. Strogatz's synthesis maps the order-parameter formalism directly onto what here is being called the *phase of the cycle*. Whole-brain Hopf models — Stuart-Landau oscillators per region, bifurcation parameter as activation threshold, complex-valued formulation handling amplitude and phase together — are useful methodological references for moving between graph structure and continuous dynamics.

Classical Kuramoto and the Strogatz synthesis assume *fixed coupling* among oscillators with a *distribution of natural frequencies*. The substrate specifies neither: coupling is Hebbian-evolving, natural frequencies themselves drift under use. The fitting regime is the adaptive-network-of-oscillators line — Aoki & Aoyagi's co-evolving coupling-phase systems, the Berner et al. review of the broader literature. In that regime the synchronization transition is not the textbook picture; you get cluster states, frequency-degree correlations, splay states, chimeras, and slow drift in the coupling matrix itself that can produce metastable cycle dynamics. Several of those phenomena — cluster states, metastable cycles — are arguably what the substrate's "cycle as field property" is reaching for. The order parameter still exists. The transition behavior and the cycle structure don't follow Strogatz off the shelf. Kuramoto + Strogatz are the right starting anchor; the adaptive-network extension is what the substrate's dynamics actually need.

The orchestration/stigmergy distinction clarifies under this framing. Hierarchical orchestration discretizes cleanly into a graph because there is in fact a discrete supervisor, a discrete edge, a discrete protocol. Stigmergic coordination does not. Modeling it as a graph means continuously fighting the abstraction. A continuous formulation should reduce back to graph-shaped behavior in the orchestration limit, rather than the other way around — the regime where the most novel and least-understood collective failure modes will appear at scale is the substrate-mediated one.

## The cycle as a field property

Cognition at every scale where it has been studied operates through recursive cycles between divergence (extraction, mapping, surfacing alternatives, holding multiple framings) and convergence (evaluation, compression, stabilization on a frame, action). The neural-scale anchor is neuronal criticality: subcritical regimes where activity dies out (lock-in to silence, the convergence trap), supercritical regimes where activity spreads everywhere (drift, the divergence trap), and critical regimes characterized by scale-free cascades and 1/f spectra (Beggs & Plenz 2003; Shew & Plenz 2013; Cocchi et al. 2017 for the review). The individual-cognitive-scale anchor is the divergent/convergent thinking distinction (Guilford 1967; Cropley 2006). At cultural scale the case is less consolidated but argued in similar shape (Page 2007; Sawyer 2012). Neither pole is the goal at any of these scales. The pathology is loss of cycle: getting stuck in convergence (premature closure, attractor lock-in, echo) or getting stuck in divergence (drift, chaos, failure to act). There is no principled reason to expect multi-agent AI systems to be exempt.

What the substrate offers is not the discovery of this cycle but an *operational definition* of it: candidate observables and a measurement apparatus tied to them. The active region of the substrate expands and contracts. The active regions in phase space occupied by individual agents disperse and re-cohere. Gradients sharpen and flatten. Premature collective closure is an active region that contracted and stayed contracted. Failure to stabilize is one that never settles. The neuronal-avalanche literature hands you concrete order-parameter candidates worth borrowing — branching ratio σ, avalanche-size exponent τ — that slot into the same role at substrate scale. In a graph + message-passing model these phenomena are at best statistical patterns reconstructed from output traces. In a substrate-mediated model they are observations on something that exists. The contribution is the operationalization. Without the substrate, "cycle between divergence and convergence" is evocative metaphor with no agreed measurement. With it, the cycle becomes a measurement architecture, and the right test of the framing is whether its observables predict downstream collective failure (echo, drift, fragmentation) at lead time better than output-trace forensics.

The empirical lineage from current AI is *training-time* evidence motivating an *inference-time* hypothesis. Song, Kempe and Munos show that outcome-based RL improves reasoning accuracy but produces a systematic loss of generation diversity that transfers from solved to unsolved problems — optimization for convergence with structural costs at the diversity level. Li et al. argue that standard RLVR with reverse KL-divergence actively accelerates diversity collapse, and that mass-covering f-divergences used as a rehearsal mechanism mitigate it. Both interventions live at the loss function — RL gradient pressure on a single model. The substrate addresses the next layer of the stack: inference-time coupling among populations of already-trained models, where the same selection pressure shows up not in gradients but in coordination dynamics. Training-time diversity collapse is empirically established; the inference-time multi-agent analog is the article's hypothesis, and the substrate is the proposed *measurement architecture* for it. Diversity is being lost, structurally, by mechanisms that have no instrument for noticing they are losing it. The substrate, in part, is what such an instrument could look like.

## Membranes as folds, not folders

If the substrate is a continuous medium, its structure shows up as differential permeability — regions of higher and lower coupling, faster and slower flow, denser and sparser propagation. A *membrane* is exactly such a structural feature: a region of differential flow rate between two parts of the substrate.

The contrast worth holding: **folds, not folders.** Folders gate by category — discrete, syntactic, identity-bound. You are in this folder or not; this document is shared or not. Folders belong to the access layer, where the existing machinery (auth identity, reputation systems, commitment devices) is well-trodden. Folds gate by flow rate — continuous, structural, dynamic. They modulate the rate at which information propagates between regions, the density of flow under sustained attention, the gradient of activation across the boundary, the direction of flow (membranes can be asymmetric), and the temporality (membranes can be momentary, persistent, or intermittent depending on the coupling structure that produces them). Folders and folds are not in competition; they are different layers of the stack.

As an intervention surface, modulating the density of flow across a membrane constitutes a structural intervention on the multi-agent dynamic. No agent is overridden, no output is filtered, no supervisor inserted. The rate at which information propagates between two regions of the shared substrate changes, and the coordination dynamic between the agents coupled through that membrane changes accordingly. The intervention happens to the medium, not to the agents. This is a structurally different class of safety lever than current oversight architectures consider — predominantly behavioral approaches that observe outputs, intervene on outputs, constrain outputs. Substrate-level intervention is structural: observe field properties, intervene on field properties, shape the medium. The intervention is upstream of behavior.

The lever runs in both directions. Lowering coupling between regions creates room for divergence — agents stop reinforcing each other's frames, the active field broadens, exploratory capacity returns. Raising coupling promotes convergence — shared frames accumulate, agents stabilize on a coherent position. Neither move is intrinsically good. A network stuck in echo wants attenuation; a network unable to coordinate wants amplification. Substrate-level transparency is what makes that judgment tractable in principle, before the intervention is selected rather than after.

This places the substrate-level membrane in the lineage Meadows traced through her *Leverage Points*. Worth naming the rank rather than gesturing at "high-leverage" generally. Modulating the density of information flow between subsystems sits at #6 in Meadows' twelve-rank taxonomy (the structure of information flows), and touches #4 (self-organization — the system's capacity to evolve its own structure). Both are mid-tier. They sit *above* parameter-tuning (#12), buffer sizing (#11), and goal-injection at the agent layer — which is exactly the comparison the substrate-vs-behavioral-oversight argument turns on. They sit *below* paradigm-change (#2) and transcendence-of-paradigms (#1). The substrate doesn't claim either. The claim is that #6 and #4 are structurally upstream of the layer current oversight architectures operate at, and that the multi-agent AI regime hasn't had a tractable instrumentation for them. The same architectural surface that enables observability provides the lever for intervention. Not two architectures stitched together; one architectural commitment.

## What this opens

This is the sketch of an architectural commitment, not a finished system. The empirical work is identifying the right field observables; building formalisms — likely in the adaptive-oscillator-network family rather than the fixed-coupling Kuramoto family — that yield small sets of order parameters meaningfully characterizing the phase of the cycle; characterizing the operators for bidirectional intervention; understanding how the signal degrades or compounds across nesting depth. The history of dynamical-systems methods, coupled-oscillator analysis, neuronal-criticality measurement, and field-based models of distributed cognitive activity gives a usable starting toolkit. None of it is presumed to be exactly the right instrument; what is presumed is the category.

Four open questions fall out. *Observability of cycles:* what are the right field-level observables for the divergence/convergence cycle in a multi-agent substrate, and which of them surface premature closure or failure to stabilize earlier than agent-behavioral evaluation? *Formalism for cycles:* can adaptive-network formalisms yield a small set of order parameters that meaningfully characterize the phase of the cycle — exploration, stabilization, lock-in, drift — across different agent populations and task domains, mapping onto the collective failure modes the field is increasingly concerned about? *Bidirectional intervention:* can density-modulation interventions on substrate membranes restore the cycle when it is stuck, without imposing top-down control or overriding individual agents — and what does the safety guarantee on such interventions look like compared to the guarantees obtainable for behavioral oversight? *Fractal observability:* how does cycle observability compound in a recursively nested system; does the signal degrade with depth, and what does that imply for the depth of multi-agent hierarchies that can be governed via substrate-level instrumentation?

What the substrate frame opens, more than anything, is the question of where to look. As long as multi-agent AI is studied through the diagram — through which agent passed which message to which other — the most important things about it will continue to register as noise. Treat the medium as a field with its own dynamics, and the things that have been hard to measure become measurable, and the things that have been hard to intervene on become governable.
