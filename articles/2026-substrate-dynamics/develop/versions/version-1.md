## Where this comes from

Most AI systems today are built around discrete events. A query arrives, a response is generated, the system goes quiet. The infrastructure we've built — query/response APIs, single-turn evaluations, retrieval pipelines that fire only when called — assumes this rhythm.

That rhythm is ending. The current generation of AI systems is moving toward continuous information exchange among always-on agents — agents that don't wait to be queried, that maintain ongoing state, that act on each other's activity as it happens rather than at well-defined turn boundaries. The shift is already underway in production systems; it will be the default within a year or two.

I've spent the past year building memory infrastructure for AI agents (the [RAGE](/concepts/rage) project). When you build memory systems and then watch them operate in multi-agent settings, something becomes clear quickly: the dominant paradigms for *observing* these systems are also discrete-regime artefacts. Current approaches are essentially trace-based — capture the agent's outputs as a record, run post-hoc analysis over the trace, extract patterns from what was said and done. Most active work in this space focuses on decision provenance (reconstructing why a given agent took a given action) and aggregation of session-level traces into "learnings" — distilled artefacts that get fed back into subsequent sessions. This is observability as forensics: examining the record of what happened after the fact. It is useful, and it should continue. But it is structurally limited to the discrete regime — the things it can see are the things that left a record in agent outputs — and the systems being deployed today are already leaving that regime. A continuous medium needs continuous instrumentation, embedded in the medium rather than reconstructed from the trace.

This article is about what becomes visible when you stop treating memory as storage and start treating it as substrate. It builds on a longer argument I've been making across [Divergence Engines](/articles/divergence-engines) (about why similarity-optimized retrieval architectures collapse into sameness) and [How We May Think We Think](/articles/how-we-may-think-we-think) (about cognition as recursive cycles between divergence and convergence). What I'm developing here is the substrate-level instrumentation that the systems-thinking tradition's existing methods would need to reach inside the multi-agent AI regime — and the operators to intervene in the dynamics that surfaces.

## The question worth asking

The framing this article works toward, as a single question:

> How do we detect when multi-agent AI systems — across the spectrum from hierarchical orchestration to genuinely stigmergic swarms — lose the capacity to *cycle* productively between divergence and convergence: collapsing prematurely into narrow collective attractors that no individual agent was designed to seek, or failing to stabilize coherently at all? And can we design oversight *and intervention* architectures that make these cycles legible and governable as structural properties of the substrate through which the agents coordinate?

Four moves are folded into that question, each one pushing against a default in current discourse. Worth naming them upfront.

**Collective, not singular.** The failure mode being addressed is what happens to a *network* of agents reading and writing into shared context, not what happens inside a single model. The most consequential pathologies in multi-agent AI are collective — they emerge across populations of agents even when no individual agent is "trying" to cause them. Single-agent observability misses them by construction.

**Hierarchies and stigmergic swarms, both.** Most production "multi-agent" systems today are hierarchical orchestration with swarm vocabulary attached — supervisor agents, orchestrator-worker patterns, manager-coordinated specialists. Genuinely stigmergic coordination — agents acting on traces in a shared medium rather than via direct messaging — is rare in current software. It is also the regime where the most novel and least-understood collective failure modes will appear at scale. The hierarchical pathologies are relatively well-characterized: chain-of-command failures, supervisor-bottleneck collapse, principal-agent drift, the supervisor itself becoming a single point of failure in the cognitive system. The substrate-mediated regime is where the framework being developed here has the most to add. The framing covers both regimes deliberately, because real systems will live across the spectrum.

**Cycles, not divergence alone.** It's tempting to frame the goal as "preserve divergence" — that's where the Divergence Engines arguments spent most of their air. But it's one-sided. The pathology that needs surfacing is *loss of cycle*: systems that lock into either pole. Premature attractor closure is the more familiar failure (echo, mode collapse, lock-in to undesirable equilibria), but unmoored drift is a real failure mode in unconstrained multi-agent settings. The structural property worth preserving is the *capacity to oscillate* between exploration and stabilization. This matches the dynamic that distinguishes generative cognition from pathological cognition at every scale where it has been studied — neural, individual, cultural — and there is no principled reason to expect multi-agent systems to be exempt.

**Oversight and intervention together.** Detection-only is a measurement framework, not an architecture. Observability without governance is a viewing apparatus, not a system you can keep healthy. *Governable* rather than merely *intervenable* places the work in the cybernetic tradition of regulation through feedback rather than top-down override — the lever is continuous and structural, not an injection or a kill switch.

## How a knowledge substrate works

What follows is a working set of concepts under examination, not a settled architecture. Whether and how each holds up empirically is part of what would need to be tested. The framing is deliberately systems-shaped: each primitive is introduced by what it would have to do for a complex adaptive system to be observable and governable through it.

**Frames as the recursive primitive.** A complex adaptive system that nests at multiple scales (claim → session → territory → user → agent population → community) needs a single composable unit, or its dynamics have to be modeled separately at each level of nesting and substrate-wide properties become unobservable across scales. The frame, in this concept, is that unit. A frame is a typed semantic unit with slots and connections — claims, evidence, methods, decisions, problems, whatever the content makes available. Its more important property is that it composes recursively. The frame is the substrate's atom, its molecule, and its galaxy: a session contains frames but is itself a frame; a territory contains frames but is itself a frame; users, agents, and content are all instances of the same composition mechanism. This recursive uniformity is what would make the substrate fractal in the technical sense (same dynamics at every scale of nesting), and it is the precondition for the fractal observability claim in the open questions below. It is also what removes the need for special-cased "user layer," "session layer," or "agent layer" logic — the substrate runs one set of dynamics through one substance.

This is not an arbitrary modeling choice; it follows from what these entities actually share. What enables a "user" to act, what enables an "agent" to act, what makes a "report" useful, what makes a "paragraph" coherent — in all four cases, what is doing the work is a *world model*: a combined, accumulated, interconnected set of abstractions of experience. A paragraph is a small, mostly static world model frozen in language. A report is a larger, aggregated world model synthesizing many such fragments. An agent is a more active world model that updates as it interacts. A human is the most active and most embodied world model in the system. The differences between them are scale, dynamism, and embodiment — not kind. They are all configurations of accumulated abstractions of experience, varying in scope and degree of activity. Treating them as instances of one composition mechanism is what makes substrate-wide dynamics tractable; it is also what makes the framing scale-invariant in the technical sense, rather than merely analogous across scales.

**Attention and action as the energy gradient.** Complex adaptive systems require differential salience — some material has to be more present, more reachable, more influential than other material at any given moment, or the system has nothing to act on. In a substrate, attention and action together generate that gradient: what gets attended to gets activated; what gets acted on gets activated and reinforced; what receives neither decays. The gradient of activation across the substrate is what makes the substrate navigable, and what propagates state from one region to another. Attention without action is observation; action without attention is reflex. The substrate would need both, and the joint gradient they generate is the field over which everything else plays out.

**Co-attention and co-action as coupling.** Complex adaptive systems develop structure through feedback loops, and one of the most fundamental positive-feedback loops in cognitive systems is reinforcement on co-activation: elements that are jointly active strengthen their coupling, which makes joint activation more likely next time, which strengthens the coupling further. This is the Hebbian loop ("fire together, wire together") — a specific positive-feedback dynamic well-suited to substrate-level emergence of structure. In the substrate, this would show up as coupling strength: frames attended to or acted on together accumulate mutual influence; the strength grows with use and decays without it. The coupling is not a static edge added to a graph but a dynamic strength that ebbs and flows under continuous use. Any "graph" describing the substrate at a given moment is a snapshot of an underlying continuous process, not the process itself. This is what would make the substrate's structure emergent from interaction rather than imposed by schema. It is also what creates the conditions under which sub-populations of agents develop shared frames without explicit coordination — the precondition for stigmergic coordination becoming tractable in software.

**Phase signatures as emergent identity.** A complex adaptive system needs a way to distinguish populations that are mutually intelligible from populations that are not, without imposing identity by fiat. Agents with similar signatures coordinate through the substrate without explicit messaging. Agents with orthogonal signatures cannot easily exchange context, even when nominally in the same conversation. Identity in this concept is something the substrate produces, not something it requires.

**Decay as differential salience over time.** Without principled forgetting, every signal eventually equals every other signal — no gradient, no navigability, no system. Taken together, these concepts describe a working hypothesis of a substrate as a continuous medium with its own physics: dynamic, emergent, observable as a *field* rather than as a list of records.

The formal lineage worth naming is the line of work that treats distributed cognitive activity as field dynamics over a structured medium, rather than as discrete signals over a graph. The most direct empirical anchor in neuroscience is Atasoy and colleagues' connectome-harmonics framework, where eigenmodes of the structural connectome's graph Laplacian provide a natural basis for cortical activity patterns. Whether and how that idea extends cleanly to multi-agent AI substrates is open, but it is the closest existing empirical analog to what the substrate concept proposes, and it is the line that has most directly shaped the working concepts above. There is also a natural intersection here with the active-inference / free-energy line of research — both bodies of work treat coordination under coupling as a phenomenon for which continuous-mathematical languages exist, and the substrate offers a candidate physical layer at which both could be operationalized. None of this presumes which formalism will turn out to be the right one for multi-agent AI; that is part of the open work.

## Three framings the substrate concept invites

### 1. Push, not pull: continuous availability with attention-driven propagation

Most current "AI memory" systems treat retrieval as the primary verb. A query reaches in, finds, returns. Even sophisticated graph-based systems still operate on this rhythm: query → traverse → retrieve → respond. The substrate is the destination of an action originating outside it.

A substrate-mediated approach inverts this. Knowledge is continuously available at varying intensities — a dense interconnected field of past activation, all of it alive at some level, with attention gradients propagating material toward where it is needed. Context arrives pre-assembled. Related material surfaces ahead of explicit request. Decay performs the function relevance ranking performs in pull-based systems.

This is a different relationship between the agent and the medium than the pull regime supports. A pull-based system delivers answers. A substrate provides ambient cognitive context — associated material continuously informing what the agent does next, without the agent having to request it. The agent is not standing outside a store and reaching in; it is immersed in a medium that is continuously offering material at the periphery of its attention, with the gradient sharpening or softening as the agent moves through the field.

The implication for multi-agent settings is structural. When N agents share a substrate, push-not-pull means they are not querying the same store independently — they are immersed in a shared cognitive field. Activation by one agent raises ambient salience for others. Decay for one is decay for the field. Coordination becomes implicit in the medium rather than something the agents perform via explicit message exchange.

This is also the regime in which the divergence/convergence cycle could become a meaningful first-class observable. A push-based substrate would exhibit cycle dynamics as field properties; a pull-based architecture renders the same dynamics statistical at best.

### 2. Inter-agent coordination as a continuous coupled dynamical system, not a graph

The dominant abstraction for multi-agent coordination — across orchestrators, supervisor patterns, agent graphs, message-passing protocols — is graph-shaped. Agents are nodes, messages are edges, coordination is traffic. Even sophisticated current work (hypergraphs, temporal graphs, dynamic graphs) operates within this regime: discrete nodes, discrete edges, discrete events.

For the failure modes most worth caring about at the collective level, this is the wrong abstraction.

A substrate-mediated multi-agent system is more accurately described as a continuous coupled dynamical system — closer in character to a coupled-oscillator network or a fluid than to a graph. Agents occupy positions in a phase space. Information has density gradients. Coordination is field interaction: phase coherence, flow patterns, attractor formation. The dynamics that matter — synchronization, lock-in, propagation cascades, drift, competitive dynamics among sub-populations — are dynamical phenomena, with natural descriptions in the language of fields rather than edges.

This shift carries methodological consequences. The appropriate toolkit moves from graph algorithms toward the broader family of methods built for continuous coupled systems — the lineage of dynamical-systems analysis applied to fields, oscillators, and flows rather than to nodes and edges. Which specific formalisms turn out to be the right ones for multi-agent AI substrates is part of the open work; what the framing commits to is the *category* of tool, not the instrument.

This also clarifies the orchestration/stigmergy distinction. Hierarchical orchestration is reasonably modeled as a graph: there is in fact a supervisor node, in fact an edge to each worker, in fact a discrete message-passing protocol. The graph is the right discretization for that regime. Stigmergic coordination — agents acting on traces in a shared medium — is not. Modeling it as a graph means continuously fighting the abstraction. The framing here treats dynamical-systems abstractions as primary on the reasoning that the regime where collective failure modes are most novel and least understood is the stigmergic one, and that a continuous formulation should reduce back to graph-shaped behavior in the orchestration limit rather than the other way around.

### 3. Membranes as coordination primitive — modulating the density of flow

If the substrate is a continuous medium, its structure shows up as differential permeability — regions of higher and lower coupling, faster and slower flow, denser and sparser propagation. A *membrane*, in this framing, is exactly such a structural feature: a region of differential flow rate between two parts of the substrate.

The contrast worth holding: **folds, not folders.** Folders gate by category — discrete, syntactic, identity-bound. Folds gate by flow rate — continuous, structural, dynamic. Authentication boundaries and binary share/don't-share gates are folders, and they belong to a different layer (closer to identity and policy) which is well-trodden territory in trust infrastructure (auth identity, reputation systems, commitment devices). Membranes in the substrate sense are folds. The membrane is the unit of the coordination layer; the folder is the unit of the access layer; they are not in competition, they are different layers.

As coordination primitives, membranes shape:

- the *rate* at which information propagates between regions
- the *density* of flow under sustained attention
- the *gradient* of activation across the boundary
- the *direction* of flow (membranes can be asymmetric)
- the *temporality* — membranes can be momentary, persistent, or intermittent depending on the coupling structure that produces them

Modulating the density of flow across a membrane constitutes a structural intervention on the multi-agent dynamic. No agent is overridden, no output is filtered, no supervisor inserted. The rate at which information propagates between two regions of the shared substrate changes, and the coordination dynamic between the agents coupled through that membrane changes accordingly. The intervention happens to the medium, not to the agents.

The same intervention surface should operate in both phases of the cycle. Lowering coupling between regions would create room for divergence — agents would stop reinforcing each other's frames, the active field would broaden, exploratory capacity would return. Raising coupling would promote convergence — agents would accumulate shared frames and stabilize on a coherent position. Neither move is intrinsically good; which one applies depends on the current pathology. A network stuck in echo wants attenuation. A network unable to coordinate wants amplification. Substrate-level transparency is what would make that judgment tractable in principle: the pathology being addressed visible before the intervention is selected. Whether the dynamics behave this way in implemented systems at scale is one of the open empirical questions.

This is a structurally different class of safety lever than current oversight architectures consider. Existing approaches are predominantly behavioral — observe outputs, intervene on outputs, constrain outputs. Substrate-level intervention is structural — observe field properties, intervene on field properties, shape the medium rather than the agent. The intervention is upstream of behavior.

It is also the natural complement to substrate-level transparency. Substrate-level state is observable in ways individual-agent introspection is not: aggregate phase, density gradients, attention distributions, dispersion-and-cohesion patterns are field properties measurable without watching every agent. The same architectural surface that enables observability provides the lever for intervention. They are not two architectures stitched together; they are one architectural commitment.

## Open territory

The substrate framing is more than a technical preference. It is what makes the central question's terms operationalizable. "Cycle between divergence and convergence," "loss of cycle," "structural property of the substrate" — none of these are objects available for measurement or intervention inside a graph + message-passing model. They become first-class once the substrate is approached as a continuous medium with its own dynamics.

Four open questions fall out:

1. **Observability of cycles.** What are the right field-level observables for the divergence/convergence cycle in a multi-agent substrate? Which surface premature collective closure, or failure to stabilize, earlier than agent-behavioral evaluation?

2. **Formalism for cycles.** Can a dynamical-systems formalism for multi-agent substrates yield a small set of order parameters that meaningfully characterize the *phase* of the cycle — exploration, stabilization, lock-in, drift — across different agent populations and task domains, and that map onto the collective failure modes (competitive drift, collusion-like coordination, lock-in to undesirable equilibria) that the field is increasingly concerned about?

3. **Bidirectional intervention.** Can density-modulation interventions on substrate membranes restore the cycle when it is stuck — pushing toward divergence when the system has prematurely converged, pushing toward stabilization when the system cannot settle — without imposing top-down control or overriding individual agents? What does the safety guarantee on such interventions look like, and how does it differ from the guarantees obtainable for behavioral oversight?

4. **Fractal observability.** How does cycle observability compound in a recursively nested system? In a hierarchical orchestration architecture, a sub-agent of a sub-agent of a sub-agent that veers off into circular reasoning or premature lock-in is structurally invisible to the levels above — there is no observability surface that crosses the orchestration boundary. In a substrate-mediated architecture with recursive uniformity at the primitive layer, the same pathology should be observable as a closed circuit in the substrate field, regardless of nesting depth. What does this look like formally? Does the signal degrade with depth, and if so, how — and what does that imply for the depth of multi-agent hierarchies that can be governed via substrate-level instrumentation?

These are research directions, not finished claims. Identifying the right observables, building the right formalism, characterizing the right intervention operators — that is the work the framing opens up rather than the work it does. What this article commits to is the *frame* in which these questions become well-posed.
