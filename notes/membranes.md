Membranes are the boundaries the substrate actually works through. A membrane is not a container or a tier — it is a boundary drawn at a moment over whatever is co-active: the agents and the frames they are reading and writing together, surrounded by sparser coupling. Interventions act here, on the membrane, rather than on the agents inside it.

The lineage is older than this project. It begins with [the dark forest](https://www.julianfleck.net/articles/spirits-dark-forest) and the wish for *selectively permeable* channels — ones that "unfold and envelop certain people in one moment and then fold back into a tight-knit sphere for the next message." A membrane is that channel, drawn over a knowledge substrate instead of a social graph.

## Membranes over membranes

The thing to read is not a cluster. Clusters partition — each node lands in one group. Membranes nest and overlap: one can sit inside another, and two can share frames without either containing the other. An agent's active context, a project's working set, and a team's shared region are all membranes at different [[scale|scales]], drawn at the same moment over overlapping material. So what we are looking at is membranes over membranes — boundaries layered and crossing, not tiles.

## The loop that forms them

One turn is a small loop. Co-attention — an agent reading, then writing, over a set of frames — does two things at once. It **aligns phase** across those frames and the agent, which *is* the membrane forming. And it **modulates the requisite variety** inside the boundary: how much, and how varied, the context in play is (cf. [[frame-type-diversity]]).

That variety sets the ceiling on what can happen next. Narrow context, a limited degree of reasoning, limited agency; broader inputs, broader reasoning, more room to act. The agent acts — and acting is just closing the loop, writing back — and the next turn's co-attention reshapes the boundary. Read and write are the same kind of event: both trigger alignment. **Decay** runs against alignment and steers the *release* of a membrane; without it, boundaries would harden and never reopen.

## What a membrane regulates

This is where my reading parts from the access-control one. In Kenneth Bruskiewicz's *Mythic Membranes* proposal ([Atlas Research Group](https://atlasresear.ch)), a membrane is semi-permeable in the sense of scoped, caveated, revertible *access* — closer to an ACL: who may see what, granted in small reversible amounts. That is a real axis, and a useful one. The model here regulates a different one: a membrane modulates the **density** of information flow — the requisite variety crossing in and out — more than the identity of who is admitted. The two are complementary; the open question is which axis is load-bearing for which problem.

A consequence worth stating: in the density reading, **disclosure policies and decision rights are emergent, not primitives**. You do not mostly set who-decides from the bottom up; the boundary's permeability and the variety it admits produce an effective distribution of disclosure and authority. Where that distribution comes out wrong, the lever is the membrane — its permeability, its decay — not the agents.
